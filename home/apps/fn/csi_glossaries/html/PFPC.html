A

 

Acceleration Clause

A clause in a mortgage contract that enables the lender to realize on a mortgage contract if there is a default of any installment or any breach of the borrower's obligation.

 

Acceptance

A contract is made once an offer has been accepted by the offeree and communicated to the offeror. Acceptance must be unconditional or it would be treated as a counter offer.

 

Access

Access is the right of a child to maintain a relationship with the non-custodial parent. It is the child's right, not the right of the custodial or non-custodial parent.

 

Accident

In liability insurance contracts coverage is either on an accident basis or an occurrence basis. For coverage on an accident basis, the injury or damage must be due to an event that is sudden, unintended and unexpected.

 

Accident Benefits

A type of automobile insurance coverage that pays medical, death and total disability benefits without regard to fault. Property damage is excluded.

 

Accidental Death Benefit -- ADB

A life insurance rider that allows for additional coverage if the insurer's death is accidental.

 

Activities of Daily Living -- ADL

Eligibility for long term care benefits is measured on the insurer's ability or inability to perform the activities of daily living (ADLs) such as bathing, dressing, toiletry, and eating.

 

Adjusted Cost Base -- ACB

Adjusted Cost Base

 

Administrator

See Executor

 

Adverse Possession

A means of acquiring title to real estate through occupancy of the property (similar to squatters rights). The Torrens or Land Title System eliminates this risk.

 

Adverse Selection

Insurance-Individuals with significant health problems or dangerous occupations are attracted to insurance. For example, an individual suffering a heart attack is more liable to purchase life insurance than healthy individuals. Insurance companies recognize this tendency and either charge higher premiums, limiting coverage or refusing to insure.

 

Age of Majority

a.k.a. legal age. An age that varies from province to province, where individuals can enter into legal binding contracts. It is 18 in all provinces except BC, NS, NB, NFLD, Yukon and NWT.

 

Agency

A type of relationship that exists between two parties where one party-the agent-acts on behalf of another party-the principal. The agent can enter into agreements with other parties on behalf of the principal. However, the agent has a fiduciary responsibility to act in the principal's best interest.

 

Agent of Necessity

A person that has no actual authority, nor apparent authority can enter into contractual arrangements for the necessities of life. For example, a deserted spouse can charge for food.

 

Aleatory Contract

A type of contract whereby the values exchanged are not equal and the performance of which is dependent upon the occurrence of a contingent event. For example, in an insurance contract the premium is usually lower than the coverage for the loss.

 

Alimony

Alimony is usually the term used for financial spousal support where the marriage still exists (as in the case of judicial separation or pending divorce). Maintenance is the term associated with financial support payments after the divorce decree.

 

All Perils

A type of automobile insurance. This broad coverage includes collision, comprehensive and specific perils.

 

Alpha

A measure of a security's return that is not directly associated with changes in the market. It justifies the need for fundamental analysis.

 

Alternative Minimum Tax -- AMT

This minimum tax was enacted as a political solution to the perception that many high-income taxpayers were paying little tax through the use of tax shelters and other so-called tax preferences. It is relatively narrow in focus, concentrating on specific shelters and credits. The minimum tax is an alternative tax calculation. You must calculate your tax both normally and under the minimum tax rules, and pay the higher of the two.

 

Amortization

Gradually writing-off the value of an intangible asset over a period of time. Commonly applied to items such as goodwill, improvements to leased premises, or expenses of a new stock or bond issue.

 

Annuities

The concept is to take a lump sum and convert it to a cash flow. As a retirement option a client can convert an RRSP to a series of cash flows. When received these cash flows are taxed in the hands of the recipient. The purchaser of the annuity is known as the Annuitant and the company selling the annuity is known as the Issuer

 

Annulment

a.k.a. nullity- A court decree that voids a marriage on the basis of a fact existing at the time of marriage. For example, one party was underage or one of the parties was already married.

 

Any Occupation

A disability definition whereby after receiving benefits for 2 years, if the insured is able to perform any occupation which he or she is suited for by way of education, training or experience, the insurer can stop benefits.

 

Apparent Authority

An agency relationship is established when a person (principal) allows another person to act on their behalf.

 

Asset Allocation

The mixture in a portfolio between cash or near cash securities, fixed income securities and equities. The proportions held are determined by client's propensity for risk.

 

At Risk Rules

An investor in a limited partnership cannot claim business losses from the partnership in excess of the amount invested, or the amount placed at risk of loses.

 

Attribution Rules

Revenue Canada rules that state that income (and in the case of a spouse, capital gains) from the transfer of property be taxed in the hands of the transferor not in the hands of the transferor's spouse or minor children.

B

 

Bargain

Often called -- meeting of the mind, the bargain represents in words offer and acceptance. A contract is fundamentally a bargain-one person does or promises to do something in return for something done or promised by the other party.

 

Before Tax Rate

A rate of return adjusted for taxes. Computed as -- After tax rate/(1-tax rate)

 

Beneficiaries

The person or persons who are to receive the deceased's assets. See also legatee

 

Beneficiary

The beneficiary is the party named to receive the benefits of an insurance policy, a will or a trust.

 

Benefit Amount

In a disability policy this is the monthly income an individual would be entitled to receive should they become disabled.

 

Benefit Period

In a disability contract this is the length of time the disability benefits are paid, usually expressed in years. The longer the benefit period the higher the disability premium.

 

Beta

A measure of non-diversifiable risk. Using linear regression analysis betas measure the relative fluctuations of a security or portfolio to a market index (TSE 300).

 

Breach of Contract

The failure of the completion of a contract because one party fails to perform what was agreed upon. A breach may result in an action for damages. A contract is breached if -- one party does something to make completion impossible (cannot lease a property as it has been sold to another party); or, one party refuses to comply; or does too little too late.

 

Broker

A securities firm or an investment advisor associated with a firm. When acting as a broker for the purchase or sale of listed stock, the investment advisor does not own the securities him or herself, but acts as an agent for the buyer or seller and charges a commission for these services.

 

Business Overhead Insurance

A type of disability insurance that pays benefits needed to cover a small business-rent, utilities and staff salaries. Typically there is a 15 to 30 day waiting period and an 18 to 24 month benefit period.

 

Buy-Sell Agreements

A shareholders or partnership agreement where, in the death or disability of either partner, the surviving partner would be required to purchase all the shares or the interest of the deceased or disabled partner-at an agreed upon price.

C

 

Cafeteria Plans

a.k.a. Flexible Benefits Plans A type of group insurance plan that allows employees to select those employee benefits that best meet specific needs. Cafeteria plans allow employees to select among various group life, medical expenses, disability and dental plans that are offered. Cafeteria plans also allow employers to introduce new benefits to meet the specific needs of certain employees.

 

Canada Deposit Insurance Corp. -- CDIC

 

Canada Interest Act

A federal statute that require the interest rate on mortgages can only be computed semi-annually, and not in advance.

 

Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation -- CMHC

A federal corporation that provides a variety of mortgage insurance programs.

 

Canada Pension Plan -- CPP

A national earnings related social insurance system providing retirement, disability and survivors benefits

 

Canadian Controlled Private Corporation -- CCPC

A corporation that is not controlled by non-residents or a public corporation. As long as this type of corporation has earned active business, and over 5 employees, it would be eligible for the small business tax deduction.

 

Canadian Life and Health Insurance Association -- CLHIA

 

Capital Asset Pricing Model -- CAPM

A model that relates the risk measured by beta to the level of expected or required return.

 

Capital Cost Allowance -- CCA

An amount allowed under the Income Tax Act to be deducted from the value of certain assets and treated as an expense in computing an individual's or company's income for a taxation year It may differ from the amount charged for the period in depreciation accounting.

 

Capital Property

Property that if sold will lead to a capital gain or loss. Includes stocks bonds and real estate.

 

Career Average Plan

A type of defined benefits plan calculated as a percentage of an employees career earnings. Employees may make a contribution to the funding of this plan.

 

Carrying Costs

Quite figuratively, the cost of carrying the mortgage and the associated costs of maintaining a house.

 

Cash Cards

A type of card issued by financial institutions and finance companies who profit from the spread between the deposit rates and the rates charged on unpaid balances (Visa, MasterCard, American Express).

 

Cash Flow

A company's net income for a stated period plus any deductions that are not paid out in actual cash, such as depreciation, amortization, deferred income taxes and minority interest. Cash flow can provide a broader picture of a company's earning power than net earnings alone. Cash flow is important to investors as it shows the company's ability to pay dividends and finance expansion.

 

Cash Surrender

A non-forfeiture option whereby the accumulated cash in an insurance contract can be withdrawn.

 

Certificate of Appointment of Estate Trustee

A document (provided from a will) provided by the Ontario courts that allow an executor to transfer property from the deceased's estate to a beneficiary.

 

Certified Financial Planner -- CFP

 

Certified General Accountant -- CGA

 

Certified Management Accountant -- CMA

 

Charge Accounts

A type of account offered by retailers that allow an individual to pay for a good or service at a later date.

 

Charitable Trust

A trust set up for the benefit of the community. In Quebec it is called a social trust.

 

Child Term Rider

A type of life insurance rider that allows for additional term coverage for all the policyholder's children.

 

Civil Code

A law system used in the province of Quebec, based on Roman law from the Justinian Code.

 

Closet Indexing

Closet indexing means building a portfolio that's close enough to a performance benchmark such as the TSE 300 that the manager never under performs by much. However, closet indexing also means managers never beat a benchmark by much, which means that investors aren't getting much value added.

 

Codicil

Amendments to a will.

 

Cohabitation Agreements

A type of contract that defines rights and obligations with respect to ownership of property and the division of such property in the event of separation of couples living together but not married. This can apply to both heterosexual couples and same sex couples.

 

Coinsurance

An insurance clause designed to encourage the insured to carry adequate insurance on their property. Unless the insured carries a stipulated percentage, a portion of the loss is shared.

 

Collateral

An asset that is used to secure a debt obligation. Failure to repay means that the asset can be seized and sold, with the proceeds going to pay off the debt.

 

Collision and Upset

A type of automobile insurance coverage that covers damages from running into something.

 

Commercial Trust

A type of Quebec trust established to provide benefits to the settlor. RRSP trusts are an example.

 

Common Law

A law system with rules established by court decisions-on judicial precedence. Originated in England and adopted in Canada.

 

Competence

Principle 3 of the Code of Ethics and Standards of Conduct for Financial Planners states that the financial planner will meet a required standard of education, pull in professionals as required and maintain ongoing educational enlightenment (like that?).

 

Comprehensive

A type of automobile insurance coverage against everything except collision and upset.

 

Conditional Sales Contract

type of contract whereby the seller retains title to the asset until the purchaser repays the full amount of the purchase price.

 

Condonation and Connivance

One of the three prohibitions or bars to divorce. Condonation and connivance means that a partner cannot go back to their partner's past transgressions and use them as the basis for a divorce, when their actions and behaviour demonstrates forgiveness.

 

Confidentiality

Principle 5 of the Code of Ethics and Standards of Conduct for Financial Planners, which states that information received from the client is privileged and cannot be released without authorization.

 

Consideration

Something of value (a valuable consideration, not a gift) given for the performance of a promise. It must be for something done now or to be performed in the future-it cannot be for something done in the past, for something illegal, something done for free, or for something as vague as love and affection. The adequacy (fairness) of consideration is not relevant, as it would place an unreasonable burden on the court to rule upon an intangible.

 

Constructive Trust

A type of trust imposed by the courts to redress imbalances between parties. For example, one individual contributes work while a second party contributes only money to purchase an asset. The cash contributor argues that the asset belonged to him as he contributed the cash. The courts may rule that there was a joint intention of both parties to work towards the acquisition of the asset and therefore the asset belongs to both.

 

Consumer Price Index -- CPI

A major inflation measure computed by Statistics Canada. It measures the change in prices of a fixed basket of a variety of goods and services in the previous month. This basket of goods is supposed to reflect the average needs of a Canadian family.

 

Contingent Beneficiary

A second person who would receive the benefits of an insurance policy if the primary beneficiary dies before the insured. See Primary Beneficiary.

 

Continuing or Enduring Power of Attorney

Terms that must be put in place in a Power of Attorney to give an individual the powers to act on behalf of a mentally incompetent person. Failure to have these may mean that the interested parties may have to apply to he courts for authority.

 

Contract

An agreement between two or more parties to do something for a price or a promise to do something. The result is an agreement that can be enforced by the courts.

 

Contract Under Seal

A formal method of committing to a contract. Each incorporated company has a seal that formalizes its intent or purpose to create a legal relationship.

 

Contracts of Adhesion

In insurance contracts, any ambiguities and uncertainties are normally held against the insurance company.

 

Conventional Mortgage

A type of mortgage whereby the loan does not exceed 75 percent of the appraised or purchase price, whichever is less.

 

Conversion Privilege

A clause in a group life insurance policy that allows the employee to convert the amount of their group life coverage into an individual life insurance policy.

 

Conversion Provision

A life insurance policy provision that allows the holder to convert or exchange their policy for different coverage.

 

Corollary Relief

Corollary relief in a divorce action is all the requested relief (custody, access, support and maintenance) except divorce.

 

Cost of Living

A life insurance rider that allows the policyholder to offset increases in inflation through the purchase of additional term insurance.

 

CPP/QPP Death Benefits

A lump sum ($2,500) death benefit payable on a priority basis, to the person who paid the funeral expenses or to the contributor's estate.

 

CPP/QPP Spouses Pension

A portion of CPP/QPP pension that is paid to a surviving spouse. The dollar amount received is age dependent.

 

Credit Rating

The lender's assessment of an individual's ability to repay a loan.

 

Credit Risk

Often called business risk (equities) or default risk (fixed income securities), it refers to the degree of uncertainty associated with a company's earnings and the company's ability to pay interest and/or dividends.

 

Credit Union/Caisse Populaires

A type of financial institution owned by its depositors.

 

Criss-Cross Ownership

An arrangement whereby investors or partners hold life and disability insurance against their opposite partners or shareholders. The idea being that if one partner dies or becomes disabled, the other partner can purchase the other's interest. You get the business not the other partners or shareholder's relatives.

 

Critical Illness Insurance -- CII

A relatively new type of insurance that pays a benefit to the insured if the insured survives 30 days after the diagnoses of a critical illness (heart attack, cancer, stroke, etc,).

 

Cumulative Excess Amount -- CEA

The excess amount in an RRSP above and beyond the $2,000 over contribution limit. This amount is taxed at 1% per month.

 

Cumulative net investment losses -- CNIL

D

 

Damages

Amounts awarded to set the plaintiff in the same position as if the contract had been performed. Not a punishment and the plaintiff must mitigate his damages.

 

Decree Absolute

A court decree that finalizes the divorce-30 days after the Decree Nisi. When an individual receives a decree absolute they are free to remarry.

 

Decree Nisi

When the courts grant a divorce, the judgment is called a Decree Nisi.

 

Decree of Judicial Separation or Separation Agreement

Separation as to bed and board. Similar to divorce it deals with the division of property and responsibilities (support, access etc.) but does not alter the couple's married status.

 

Deductibles

A general insurance provision whereby the insured is responsible for any losses up to a certain limit, in return for a lower premium.

 

Deduction Room

The amount of RRSP that can be carried forward from previous years. For example if a client was entitled to place $13,500 in an RRSP and only contributed $10,000, the difference of $3,500 would be the unused contribution room.

 

Deemed Disposition

When you die Revenue Canada deems that you sold your assets the day before. The result may trigger capital gains. If the assets are jointly held deemed disposition would not result until the surviving holder died.

 

Deferred Annuities

While most annuities are purchased to provide an immediate cash flow, deferred annuities are purchased to provide a higher future cash flow.

 

Deferred Income

Income that is not realized until a future date. Taxes on this income are delayed or deferred until the income is received. RRSPs are a method of deferring income and taxes.

 

Deferred Profit Sharing Plan -- DPSP

A trust arrangement whereby an employer distributes a certain percentage of company profits to his/her employees. It must be an arms length transaction, and employees are not eligible to make a contribution.

 

Defined Benefit Pension Plan -- DB Plan

A type of industry pension plan that uses a predetermined formula to determine the dollar amount of an employee's pension.

 

Defined Contribution Pension Plan -- DC Plan

A type on industry pension plan where the amount contributed is known but the dollar amount of the pension to be received is unknown. AKA Money Purchase Plan

 

Demand Loan

A type of credit arrangement secured by collateral that can be terminated by either party.

 

Demographics

Attempt to predict the behavior of a group of clients by segmenting them into homogenous groups classified according to age, wealth, marital status, gender, income and/or occupation.

 

Devolve

Quebec succession terminology meaning the passing on of assets.

 

Diligence

Principle 7 of the Code of Ethics and Standards of Conduct for Financial Planners, which states that the financial planner shall be prompt and thorough in all dealings.

 

Direction on Short Notice

A court can order the sale of the matrimonial home if the couple cannot come to terms on what is a reasonable price.

 

Discretionary Encroachment Clause

A clause in a will that gives the executor authority to sell assets for the benefit of an underage beneficiary.

 

Diversification

In order to reduce risk investors purchase different types of securities and securities across industries. As long as the securities are not perfectly positive, combining securities reduces, but does not eliminate risk.

 

Doctrine of Frustration

According to this doctrine a contract is unenforceable by either party when, without the fault of either party, the contractual obligations cannot be performed. A change in circumstances that make it difficult for one party to complete the contract does not necessarily invalidate the contract (Example -- an increase in interest rates making the purchase of a house more expensive, does not invalidate the contract to purchase the house.). The doctrine applies if performance is made impossible by a change in law; or, the thing upon which the contract is based does not exist; or the performance of a personal contract becomes impossible.

 

Doctrine of Unconscionable Bargain

A doctrine that states that a contract can be rendered unenforceable if it is established that one of the parties to the contract through weakness or necessity was unable to protect themselves and the bargain was grossly unfair.

 

Domestic Contracts

A contract between two married (or soon to be married) parties that specifies the division of property upon marriage breakdown. In many provinces a domestic contract is unenforceable unless it was made in writing, signed by the parties and witnessed.

 

Dower Rights

Under old common law a woman could not own property, but was entitled to one third of her spouse's estate upon his death. Dower has been eliminated in most Canadian jurisdictions.

 

Duress

An action by one party that compels another to do something he or she would not normally do. In the Criminal Code it is defined as... threats of immediate death or grievous bodily harm from a person present when the offence was committed. Contracts made under duress are unenforceable.

E

 

Easement

The right to access or use another person's property.

 

Efficient Market Hypothesis

The concept that the market price of securities always fully reflects available information and that it is difficult, if not impossible, to consistently outperform the market by picking undervalued securities.

 

Elimination Period

In a disability contract this is the period of time that the insured must be disabled before they are eligible for benefits. The longer the elimination period the lower the disability premiums.

 

Emancipated Minor

A minor who by marriage or court order is deemed to have reached the age of majority (provincial legal age). Contracts signed by emancipated minors are enforceable.

 

Eminent Domain

The right of governments to expropriate property for public use.

 

Encumbrance

Claims against an estate. Encumbrances include such items as mortgages, judgments and liens.

 

Endorsements/riders

Alterations that vary the terms on an insurance contract.

 

Equity Ratio

A ratio of net assets (assets-liabilities) to total assets. Equity ratios can be calculated on short-term assets and liabilities to measure liquidity or on investment assets and investment loans to measure leverage.

 

Errors and Omissions Insurance

A type of insurance that is used by professionals to protect themselves from errors, omissions and negligence.

 

Estate Freeze

Freezing the value of an individual's growth assets (stocks, bonds and real estate) so that the future asset growth passes to the taxpayer's children or spouse. The accrued value of the assets becomes taxable to the donor when he or she dies, or the assets are sold.

 

Estate Protector Life Insurance Policy

A life insurance policy designed to waive any further premiums on the death of the first spouse and pay a death benefit at the death of the surviving spouse. The policy proceeds are used to pay the final income taxes.

 

Exchange Rate Risk

The risk that changes in the value of Canadian dollar will affect the value of securities.

 

Executive Pension Plan

A type of IPP for those employees who are neither owners nor own more than 10 percent of the shares of a company.

 

Executor

The designated person to whom the will is entrusted, that is, the person whose duty it is to carry out the provisions of the will. The meaning is the same for -- estate trustee (PFP Text p. 10-1); administrator (PFP Text p. 10-4); executrix (female); or personal representative.

 

Extended Term

The accumulated cash value in an insurance policy is used to pay premiums for the same amount of coverage for a given period of time. It is a type of non-forfeiture option.

 

Extrapratimonial Rights

A Quebec term that describes a persons rights that have no monetary value.

F

 

Fairness

Principle 4 of the Code of Ethics and Standards of Conduct for Financial Planners, which states that the financial planner shall disclose conflicts of interest.

 

Family Asset

Property owned by one or both spouses and ordinarily used by a spouse or a minor child of either spouse for a family purpose.

 

Family Patrimony

Family or communal property in the province of Quebec. It includes the residence, household furniture, motor vehicles and benefits under a retirement plan.

 

Family Unit

The Tax Act defines this unit to include a taxpayer and spouse, other than a spouse that is legally separated and living apart, and unmarried children under 18 years of age.

 

Fiduciary Duty

An obligation of the agent to act in the best interests of the principal. The principal depends on the agent to provide the best service possible, within the agency agreement.

 

Final Average Plan

A type of defined benefits plan based on earnings over a stated period of time. For example, the best 5 consecutive years earnings in the last 10 years of employment.

 

Financial Planners Standards Council of Canada -- FPSCC

A board established to develop educational and experience criteria for the proficiency designation of CFP. Now called FPSCC.

 

Financial Planning

The business of providing a tangible financial plan recommending strategies and actions to meet specific goals. Financial planning is an ongoing process that requires monitoring and updating.

 

First Home Loan Insurance -- FHLI

A type of home insurance program for those who have not owned a principal residence within the preceding 5 years. Insurance covers up to 95%.

 

Flat Benefits Plan

A simplified defined benefits plan commonly used by unionized employees. The amount to be received is usually a negotiated amount times the number of years of service.

 

Fraudulent Misrepresentation

A misrepresentation made with the intent to deceive the party to whom it was made. Parties acting on the fraudulent advice and incurring losses may receive damages under torts (PFP Text p. 5-35).

 

Freehold

An interest in real estate without a predetermined time span.

 

Future Economic Loss -- FEL

An Ontario Workers' Compensation treatment for work related claims based on the lost wages a disabled worker forgoes. The benefit is tied to vocational rehabilitation and usually represents 90% of the wages lost.

G

 

General Anti Avoidance Rules -- GAAR

The Income Tax Act provides a general anti-avoidance rule that states that if you come up with a way of avoiding taxes, and that is not caught by the existing rules, but is a misuse or abuse of the Act, it may be caught by GAAR.

 

Giving in Payment Clause

In Quebec this a clause of the hypothec which permits the courts to award the lender (upon registration and 60 days notice) ownership of the land and the building if the default is not corrected within the 60 days.

 

Grace Period

A life insurance company is required to provide the policy holder with 31 days grace without forfeiting any contractual rights.

 

Group Life Insurance

A type of blanket insurance that covers a group of individuals within an organization. The contract for benefits is between the insurance company and the organization.

 

Guaranteed Income Supplement -- GIS

A supplement that is added to low-income pensioners who qualify for OAS. GIS is based on a person's previous year's income and also clawed back based on the person's previous year's income. GIS is tax exempt.

 

Guaranteed Insurability -- GI

A life insurance rider that allows the policyholder to increase coverage at preset times without proving evidence of insurability.

 

Guaranteed Investment

A type of term deposit offered by financial institutions that typically cannot be liquidated prior to maturity.

 

Guardian Appointment Clause

A clause found in a will that appoints an individual or individuals as the guardian for minor children.

H

 

High Ratio Mortgage

A type of mortgage whereby the loan exceeds 75 percent (but not usually more than 90 percent) of the appraised value or purchase price, whichever is less.

 

Hypothec

The Quebec equivalent of the mortgage loan. It is defined as a real right upon an immovable, made liable for the fulfillment of an obligation.

 

Hypothecary Creditor

The Quebec equivalent of a mortgage lender (mortgagee).

 

Hypothecary Debtor

The Quebec equivalent of a mortgage borrower (mortgagor).

I

 

Impaired Life Annuity

A type of annuity that is linked to a client's health. Because of a health problem supported with a doctor's note, the client receives a higher payout due to the shorter expected life span.

 

Improvident Depletion of Assets

The division of assets during a marriage. This would be done when one spouse believed that the other spouse was squandering the assets. The division of assets can only be done once.

 

Income Splitting

Income splitting occurs when income, that would be normally payable in the hands of one taxpayer is taxed in the hands of both the taxpayer and other members of his or her family.

 

Income Tax Act -- ITA

 

Incontestable Clause

An insurance company cannot challenge the information provided by the policy holder after the policy has been in force for two years. The courts, however, will render the contract unenforceable if the insurance company can prove fraud.

 

Indemnify

A basic principal of insurance that states that the insured should not be able to profit from a loss. This principal attempts to restore the insured to approximately the same financial position as existed prior to the loss. This principal does not apply to life insurance.

 

Indexed Annuity

A type of annuity where payments are tied to the cost of living.

 

Individual Health Insurance

Similar to group insurance (without the life component), a type of insurance geared to the smaller businessperson. The insurance covers such things as dental, prescription drugs and travel coverage. Complete medical evidence on all family members is usually required to obtain a personal (family) health insurance policy.

 

Individual Pension Plan -- IPP

A type of defined benefit pension plan specifically structured for one person. High set-up and administrative costs, higher contributions than those allowed under RRSPs usually characterize it.

 

Indoor Management Rule

When one party enters into a contract with a company's officer, it is assumed that the company's officer has the authority to make binding contracts on behalf of the company.

 

Inflation Risk

Often called purchasing power risk it refers to the fact that with rapid increases in inflation and hence interest rates, causes fixed income securities decline.

 

Injunction

A discretionary court remedy restraining one party from acting in a specific manner. A type of remedy to a breach of contract.

 

Innocent Misrepresentation

An honest mistake or belief in a fact. An innocent misrepresentation does not invalidate a contract.

 

Installment Loan

A type of loan that requires regular payments. This type of loan can have either a fixed rate, with principal and interest blended and with a set completion schedule. The loan may have a variable rate, a.k.a. floating rate with the rate of interest fluctuating with changes in the market.

 

Insurance Bureau of Canada -- IBC

 

Integrity

Principle 1 of the Code of Ethics and Standards of Conduct for Financial Planners, which means that the client's interests should always be placed before a planner's personal gain.

 

Inter Vivos

A method of transferring assets for the use of a designated beneficiary. The major advantage of this kind of trust is that the person setting up the trust may still maintain control of the assets.

 

Interest Rate Sensitive

Fixed income securities are sensitive to changes in interest rate changes. As interest rates go up the value of fixed income securities declines, and vice versa.

 

Interim Separation Agreement

A type of interim agreement between a married couple providing for the ongoing daily needs of the parties and makes provisions for such things as payment of mortgages, spousal support, custody, access and child support. This agreement can hold for a specified time period or more commonly until a final separation agreement is in place.

 

Intestate

Dying without a will. The Family Acts of each province would prevail regarding the distribution of assets.

 

Investment Discretion Clause

A clause in a will that gives the executor wider investment discretion.

 

Investment Holding Company

A Canadian controlled private corporation in which individuals hold their investments. This proves beneficial when personal tax rates exceed corporate tax rates. However if the income earned is passive the corporation is not eligible for the small business tax deduction. KPM states that as a result of changes in the tax act a new refundable tax applies and as a result .... there is generally no tax advantage to earning investment income as a corporation.

 

Invitation to Treat

An attempt to induce offers. An advertisement or a stock exchange quote is not deemed to be a valid offer, only an invitation for offers.

J

 

January Effect

A market anomaly that contends that that small capitalization stock prices tend to perform well during the month of January.

 

Jensen Index

A statistical measure of a portfolio's performance. It measures excess returns relative to the beta of the portfolio, but has the added feature that it provides a statistical test of the significance of superior performance.

 

Joint and Last Survivor

A type of annuity that continues to be paid as long as either spouse remains alive. Because the benefits cover two, the benefits are lower than that received from a single life annuity.

 

Joint and Last Survivor Pension

A type of pension arrangement where a portion of a pension, typically 60%, is paid to the surviving spouse.

 

Joint Tenancy

Land ownership by two or more individuals subject to the rights of -- Unity of Possession, Unity of Time, Unity of Title, and Unity of Interest. Upon the death of one party, the interest of the deceased automatically transfers to survivor(s) of the tenancy.

L

 

Labour Sponsored Venture Capital Corporation -- LSVCC

As an alternative to provincial stock savings plans, these plans were established to foster the development of new or young companies. Often double tax credits were offered to entice investors to invest in these risky companies.

 

Land titles or Torrens System

A land title system that guarantees that there is clear title to a property.

 

Leasehold

An interest in land on which the tenant has a lease. The tenant can borrow against the lease.

 

Legatee

A person to whom money or property is left in a will. Beneficiary is the more common term.

 

Letter of Wishes

A non-binding declaration of the settlor's wishes that often accompany a discretionary indenture, that suggests in a non-binding manner, how the administrator should administer the trust.

 

Liability Insurance

A type of insurance whereby the party making the claim is outside the insurance policy. The insured is known as the first party, the insurer is the second party, and the claimant is usually referred to as the third party.

 

Life Cycle

A theory that an individual's age tends to shape changes in investing and personal priorities.

 

Life Income Fund -- LIF

A type or retirement fund in which a LIF holder must withdraw at least a minimum amount each year and also there is limit on the maximum that can be withdrawn. Since the LIF holder cannot cash in the total amount in the LIF it is sometimes referred to as a locked-in RRIF. At age 80 in most provinces the LIF can be converted to a life annuity.

 

Life Interest in a Specific Asset Clause

A clause in a will that gives the use of an asset to an individual for life, but the title reverts to someone else when the user dies. Sometimes called management from the grave.

 

Line of Credit

A credit arrangement with a financial institution allowing a client to draw down on a set agreed upon sum.

 

Liquidator

A Quebec succession term that describes the person who identifies the heirs and passes along the assets of a deceased person.

 

Listed Personal Property

A subset of personal-use property. It specifically includes jewelry, works of art, stamps and coins. Capital gains result if the asset is sold for greater than $1,000, capital losses on listed personal property are deductible against taxable capital gains from other listed personal property.

 

Living Will

The living will is not a will. It is a document requesting that the life not be artificially prolonged if the end result is terminal (alternatively, it could also state that all efforts should be used to prolong life). The same provisions can be set out in a Health Care Power of Attorney, Continuing Power of Attorney (Ont.), Representative Agreement (BC), Mandate Given in Anticipation of Incapacity (Que.).

 

Loan Provision

A loan provision is contained in a cash value policy, which allows the policy owner to borrow against the cash value.

 

Loan-to-Value Ratio

The difference between the purchase price and the amount of the mortgage loan. A conventional or non-insured mortgage must not exceed 75% of the lessor of the value of the property or the purchase price.

 

Locked In RRSPs

A type of RRSP where funds that have been vested by an employee must be placed when an employee leaves a job. The pension funds provided by the employer are the property of the employee (vested) and must be transferred to this type of fund. At a specified age funds must be used to purchase a life annuity or a Life Income Fund.

 

Locked-In Retirement Account -- LIRA

See LIFs. An account in which locked-in funds from a registered pension plan are deposited before being transferred to a LIF at or before age 69.

 

Loco Parentis

An adult who is acting as a parent and has parental responsibilities. The courts may rule that they may have a support obligation for the child.

 

Long Term Care Insurance -- LTC

A type of insurance that pays for long term health and personal care benefits. Generally the benefits are a stated monthly amount with a lifetime maximum.

 

Loss Payable Clause

An insurance policy clause that makes the payment jointly payable to the insured and another individual or institution. For example, the loss on a vehicle may be made payable to the registered owner and the financial institution that financed the car.

M

 

Maintenance

The supply of the necessities of life (food, lodging and clothes) for a person. Upon the granting of a Decree Nisi an order of maintenance may be made requiring one spouse to pay lump sums or periodic payments for the maintenance of the other spouse and for any children of the marriage. See Alimony.

 

Managed RRSPs

In this case a fund manager manages an RRSP and the plan holder does not make subsequent investment decisions.

 

Mandates

In Quebec two Power of Attorneys (or mandates) may be required; one effective while the donor is capable, and the one that becomes effective should the donor become incapable.

 

Market out Clause

A clause that allows an underwriter to terminate an underwriting agreement.

 

Market Risk

A type of risk that is associated with equities. Often called systematic or non-diversifiable risk, it is a risk that cannot be diversified away, it is uncontrollable and affects all equities. The correction or crash of October 1987 is an example.

 

Marriage

The voluntary union between a man and a woman to the exclusion of any other person during the continuance of the union. The legal definition does not apply to same sex couples.

 

Marriage Contracts

An agreement between parties who are already married, or about to be married, dealing with the division of property in the event of a breakdown of the marriage.

 

Mentally Incompetent

Individuals who because of diminished mental capacity (insanity, under the influence of drugs or alcohol) cannot have contracts entered into enforced against them. They can however enforce the contract.

 

Misrepresentation

Words or conduct that conveys a false or misleading impression. A misrepresentation may be fraudulent, innocent or negligent.

 

Mistake

An unintentional act involving an error in the existence of a thing. A unilateral mistake is a mistake by only one party to the contract. A Unilateral contract is enforceable unless it can be shown that the other party was aware of the mistake and that by taking advantage of it would amount to fraud.

 

Money Purchase Plan -- MPP

See Defined Contribution Pension Plan

 

Morbidity

In insurance this is the risk of disability. It is commonly expressed in terms of disability per thousand per year for a specific age group.

 

Mortality

The risk of death. It is commonly expressed in terms of death per thousand per year for a specific age group.

 

Mortgage

A contract between a lender (mortgagee) and a borrower (mortgagor), whereby the borrower agrees to pledge his/her property as collateral on a loan.

 

Mortgage Backed Securities -- MBS

A type of certificate, which entitles the investor to a proportionate share of the cash flow from an underlying portfolio of mortgages.

 

Mortgage Clause

A special type of insurance condition regarding mortgages. The clause expresses an agreement between the insurer and the insured. The homeowner is obligated to notify the insurance company of the following conditions -- vacancy of the residence for over 30 days; change of ownership; increased hazard.

 

Mortgage Corporations

Subsidiaries of banks that lend money for residential mortgages.

 

Mortgage Investment Companies -- MICs

A special form of loan company administered under the Loan Companies Act established to form pools of capital for mortgages. Now defunct.

 

Mortgage Rate Protection Program

A type of CMHC insurance against increases in interest rates.

 

Myopic Loss Aversion

A behavioral finance concept which states that investors measure performance, and react to performance results, for periods shorter than their investment horizon. Investors tend to look at the short term rather than the long term.

N

 

National Housing Act -- NHA

 

Nature of the Offer

The offer in a contract must be explicit (not fuzzy); must be serious, defined (cannot go on indefinitely); revocable (can be cancelled at any time before acceptance); and concludes with the death of the creator. Example -- If a client agrees to sell his house and dies before acceptance of an offer, the deal dies with the offeror.

 

Net Worth

A personal balance sheet. Liabilities are subtracted from the value of the assets in order to establish an individual's net worth.

 

No-fault Insurance

A type of automobile insurance that pays for bodily injury and property damage without regard to fault. The objective of no-fault insurance is to eliminate the adversarial system for finding fault through the courts.

 

Nominated Contracts

A type of contract. It includes sales contracts, leases, agency mandated in the civil code and insurance contracts.

 

Non Est Factum

Refers to invalidating a contract because the signor of the contract believed he was signing another type of contract.

 

Non-Commercial Trust

A type of Quebec trust established with the intention of procuring property.

 

Non-Economic Loss -- NEL

An Ontario Workers' Compensation treatment for claims based on the evidence of permanent impairment as a result of a work related injury or disease. The payment is based on age and degree of permanent impairment. Sums equal to or less than $10,000 are paid as a lump sum, greater amounts are paid on a monthly basis over the worker's life or as a lump sum.

 

Non-forfeiture Options

If a cash value has been built up in an insurance policy this value can be used in a number of ways -- cash in the policy and take the accumulated cash value; exchange the policy's cash value for a fully paid up policy; extend the policy with no further premium payments.

 

Nullity Decree

Quebec provides that in the case of a dissolution of marriage (a court ruling known as a nullity decree) based on the bad faith of one party in contracting the marriage, the acquired property may be liquidated at the option of the innocent party, or each will take his or her property on termination of the relationship.

O

 

Objectivity

Principle 2 of the Code of Ethics and Standards of Conduct for Financial Planners, which states that the financial planner should be impartial in his/her advice.

 

Occurrence

In liability insurance contracts, coverage is either on an accident basis or an occurrence basis. For coverage on an occurrence basis, the injury or damage can occur over a period of time, but still must be unintentional and unexpected.

 

Offeree

The party who may or may not accept or be party to a contract. The opposite party in a contract.

 

Offeror

The party who makes an offer in a contract-an offer to enter into contractual arrangements.

 

Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions -- OFSI

Federal regulators for the Canadian chartered banks and the insurance industry.

 

Old Age Security -- OAS

A federal retirement program for all Canadians that meet the residency requirement. OAS is not dependent upon past income or employment. Benefits start at age 65 and are adjusted quarterly for inflation.

 

Operation of Law

A doctrine by which a contract can become unenforceable through the automatic effects of the law and not by any direct act of the parties affected. For example, a discharge from bankruptcy terminates all contracts, and lawsuits for the enforcement of breached contracts.

 

Over Contributions

An amount made in excess to the regulatory limit in an RRSP. Amounts that exceed $2,000 are penalized at a rate of 1 percent per month.

 

Own Damage

A type of automobile coverage that pays for loss or damage to the insurer's vehicle. It is not compulsory.

 

Own Occupation

A disability definition that considers the insured to be disabled if the insured is unable to perform the major duties of his or her own particular occupation.

P

 

Paper Loss

Literally a loss on paper. The value of a security or asset declines below its purchase price thereby reducing the value on an investor's portfolio. No actual loss is created unless the investor sells it at the lower price. Paper losses have no tax consequences unless the asset is sold.

 

Par

In insurance it refers to participating policies from either mutual or stock companies.

 

Participating Annuity

A type of annuity that participates in the profits, if the insurance company earns higher investment yields or has lower expenses.

 

Participating Policies

A type of insurance policy that is entitled to dividends if the company earns a surplus on its investments.

 

Past Services Pension Adjustment -- PSPA

An employer may increase a member's pension by the granting of additional past service benefits to an employee in a defined benefits plan. Plan members who incur a PSPA will have their RRSP contribution room reduced by the amount of this adjustment.

 

Patrimonies By Appropriation

A Quebec successoral planning term that describes a person's rights and obligations that do not belong to a person and is often managed by more than one person. Examples include foundations and trusts.

 

Patrimony

A Quebec term for a person's rights and obligations including assets.

 

Pension Adjustment -- PA

Any amounts paid by an employer to an employee's pension plan. This amount would be deducted from the amount that an employee could place in their RRSP. For example, if and employee calculated 18 percent of earned income and arrived at a value of $10,000 and his employer contributed $6,000 to the employee's defined contribution plan, the employee would have to deduct this $6,000 to obtain the amount that could be placed in their RRSP ($4,000).

 

Pension Benefit Standards Act -- PBSA

Private pensions must be registered either provincially or federally. If a business is under Federal jurisdiction, then their pension plan must conform to the Pension Benefits Standards Act. The federal pension act that deals with such items as eligibility for a pension, funding requirement investment disclosure and audits.

 

Pension Benefits Acts

Provincial pension legislation.

 

Personal Line of Credit

A floating rate loan with a predetermined limit. Since the loan is essentially pre-approved, the client can draw down on it at any time. Interest on a personal line of credit is calculated daily on the outstanding balance and charged monthly.

 

Personal Power of Attorney

A type of power of attorney that allows the writer (Donor) to appoint someone to make decisions of a personal nature (E.g. where the donor is to live, medical treatment to be received) in the event that the writer becomes mentally incapable.

 

Personal Services Business -- PSB

In an attempt to reduce taxes an individual may attempt to incorporate their services. However Revenue Canada will disallow the small business tax deduction if the corporation has been established to circumvent the tax laws by changing the relationship from employee to contracted company.

 

Personal Umbrella Policy

A type of insurance policy that gives the insured protection above and beyond existing policies. For example, an individual is successfully sued for $300,000 and the coverage is only $200,000 from the errors and omissions insurance. The umbrella policy would pay the additional $100,000.

 

Personal-Use Property

An all-inclusive category of personal items. A subset of personal use property is listed personal property. For both, excess received above $1,000 results in a capital gain. However, only capital losses on listed personal property (jewelry, works of art, stamps and coins) are deductible against taxable capital gains from other listed personal property.

 

Political Risk

The risk that government(s) may interfere in the economy.

 

Portfolio Management

 

Power of Attorney -- POA

A document in writing that gives another person legal authority to act on one's behalf (as an agent). The power may be general; or specific. If the document specifies that the powers to act as agent continue beyond a persons incapacity it would contain the words durable or continuous. The power as an agent comes to an end with the death of the issuer. The person giving the authority can also be called the grantor or the principal. The person appointed could also be called the attorney, the proxy or the agent.

 

Power of Attorney for Personal Care

See Living Will

 

Preferred Beneficiary Election

Under this trust mechanism, the income generated by a trust (pre 1996) could be either taxed as revenues to the trust, or in the hands of the beneficiary. This election is now only available to beneficiaries who are disabled.

 

Prescribed Annuity

A prescribed annuity is one that is purchased with after tax dollars. Since payments from a prescribed annuity are a blend of principal and interest, taxes on the interest are spread evenly.

 

Prescribed Interest Rates

Revenue Canada uses three different prescribed interest rates. The lowest rate is the Bank of Canada rate for the previous quarter and applies to attribution rules and employee and shareholder loans.

 

Presumption of Advancement

This presumption occurs when an individual purchases property and registers it in his wife's name. The presumption is that the property is a gift to the wife. The reverse, a wife purchasing property and placing it her husband's name is not a presumption of advancement. Many provinces have abolished this presumption.

 

Primary Beneficiary

The person who is first entitled to receive the proceeds of a life insurance policy.

 

Primary Mortgage Market

The name of the market where mortgages are originally created.

 

Probate

The authentication of a will.

 

Professionalism

Principle 6 of the Code of Ethics and Standards of Conduct for Financial Planners, which states that the financial planner must conduct him/herself in a manner that brings credit to the profession.

 

Prohibited degrees of consanguinity

Refers to a relationship by blood. In Canada, it is a requirement of a valid marriage that the parties not be related by blood or affinity. For example, brothers cannot marry sisters.

 

Prospect Theory

A behavioral finance concept that assumes that clients are about twice as sensitive to losses as they are to gains.

 

Provincial Stock Savings Plan -- SSPs

A provincial incentive plan to invest in new or young companies. Such plans entitle residents to tax credits or tax deductions of specified amounts through the purchase of securities of specifically designated provincial companies.

Q

 

Quebec Pension Plan -- QPP

R

 

Rate of Return

A measure of return that can be used to calculate historic returns or expected returns. To calculate rate of return an investor takes into account any cash flows (E.g. dividends, interest, rents) and any capital gains or losses divided by the amount invested or about to be invested. Rate of return is often called yield.

 

Ratification

Voluntarily adopting a contract. The adoption must be done within a reasonable time and must include the entire contract. When a minor reaches legal age he or she must either repudiate the contract within a reasonable time or the contract becomes legally binding.

 

Real Estate Investment Trusts -- REITs

Real Estate Investment Trusts are securitized investments in a portfolio of commercial real estate.

 

Real Property

The right to use real estate. These rights include the right to sell, rent or use.

 

Real Returns

The rate of return an investor would receive after the nominal or quoted rate is adjusted for inflation.

 

Recaptured Depreciation

Depreciable assets deal with real assets-buildings, cars equipment and pertain to business assets. If you claim Capital Cost Allowance the value of the asset is lowered by the amount claimed and the new value is called Undepreciated capital cost. If somewhere in the future you sell the asset for more than the Undepreciated capital cost Revenue Canada believes that you have claimed to much depreciation in prior years and you must now recapture that excess depreciation add it to income and pay taxes upon it.

 

Reduced Paid-Up Insurance

The accumulated cash value in an insurance policy is used to purchase a fully paid up policy. It is a type of non-forfeiture option.

 

Registered Educational Savings Plan -- RESP

A means of financing a child's post secondary education. Non-tax deductible contributions of $4,000 per beneficiary to a maximum of $42,000 are allowed. Funds within the plan grow tax-free. If the child does not attend the contributors can get back their principal without penalty. When the funds are withdrawn the funds are taxed in the hands of the child.

 

Registered Pension Plan -- RPP

A registered pension plan is a trust registered with Revenue Canada which is established by a company to provide pension benefits for its employees when they retire. Both employer and employee contributions to the plan are tax-deductible.

 

Registered Retirement Income Fund

A RRIF is a tax deferral vehicle available to Registered Retirement Savings Plan (RRSP) holders who deregister their plans. The plan holder invests the withdrawn RRSP funds in the RRIF and each year must withdraw and pay income tax on a set fraction of the total assets in the fund.

 

Registered Retirement Income Funds -- RIFF

An extension to a RRSP, whereby the client can maintain and manage the assets he/she accumulated in an RRSP. It if often called a reverse RRSP, as Revenue Canada requires that a stipulated amount be withdrawn each year and no further funds may be contributed.

 

Registered Retirement Savings Plan -- RRSP

A tax deferral vehicle that allows investors to make tax-deductible contributions into a trust that will be used to fund their retirement.

 

Reinstatement

Once a life insurance policy has elapsed, the policyholder has an opportunity to reinstate (renew) the policy if certain conditions are met. These include evidence of insurability, payment of all defaulted premiums, and the repayment of any outstanding loans against the policy. This has to be done within 2 years following the lapse.

 

Resulting Trust

A type of trust established by intention rather than formal documentation. For example, two individuals contribute money to an account for the eventual purchase of an asset.

 

Revolving Line of Credit

A type of line of credit that allows the client to borrow repaid amounts repeatedly.

 

Riders

Additional options added to a life insurance policy.

 

Rights or Things

Receivables to a taxpayer who reports business on a cash basis (e.g. farmers). These items are deemed to be income on the deceased's final tax return. The executor has the choice of reporting this income as a separate return or have the beneficiary report the income in his or her return.

 

Risk

In finance it would be defined as the variability of returns. The more variable or volatile the returns the greater the risk.

 

Risk Adjusted Rate of Return

The difference between the expected rate of return as calculated using CAPM and the actual rate earned.

 

Risk Aversion

Investors are usually risk averse, meaning they tend to avoid risk. However not all investors are risk averse to the same degree.

 

Risk Free Rate

The rate an investor can earn without putting his or her money at risk. The rate is the T-bill rate and is used in the market line formula.

 

Risk Return Ratio

A measure of risk per unit of return. To calculate the ratio standard deviation is divided by the geometric returns.

 

RRSP Contribution Room

The amount that an individual may put in their RRSP. It is equal to the current contribution limit, plus any carry-forward, less the PA and past service pension adjustment for the current year.

S

 

Schedule I Banks

Canadian banks that have a widely dispersed Canadian ownership. Collectively they are called the Big Six.

 

Seal

A formal method of communicating assent or intent. It may mean affixing a company seal to a document or simply the signature of a person.

 

Secondary Mortgage Market

The name of the market where mortgage securities are traded.

 

Security Market Line

A graph that demonstrates the risk return relationship between securities. The formula representing this graphical presentation is -- R= Rf + (Rm-Rf) where R is the expected return on a security or portfolio, Rf is the risk-free rate (T-bill rate), represents beta and Rm represents the historical return on the market.

 

Self Regulatory Organization SRO

Many important rules governing securities industry practices and standards in Canada are set by the self-regulatory organizations, which include the Vancouver, Alberta and Toronto Stock Exchanges, the Montreal Exchange and the Investment Dealers Association of Canada. Many of the regulatory and compliance functions have been delegated to the SROs by the provincial securities administrators.

 

Self-Directed RRSPs

Under this type of RRSP the plan holder makes the decisions as to what to purchase or hold in an RRSP. Financial assets (generally speaking) can be held but real assets are not allowed.

 

Semi-Strong Efficiency

A market is semi-strong efficient if security prices reflect all publicly available information, including published reports, announcements, and market data.

 

Settlor

The person who creates the trust.

 

Severance of Divorce from Corollary Issues

If a couple has separated for 12 months and still have unresolved issues (support, custody, access, etc.) the divorce can still be finalized with the unresolved issues settled at a later date.

 

Severe and Prolonged Disability

Under CPP/QPP this means that an individual is not able to regularly pursue any substantially gainful employment and prolonged means that the disability is likely to carry on indefinitely.

 

Shareholder Pension Plan

A type of IPP for owner-managers who own more than 10 percent of the shares of a company.

 

Sharpe Index

This measure of portfolio performance measures is similar to the Treynor Index, however in this case, standard deviation rather than beta is used. The formula is PIS,I = Ri -r

 

Short Marriage

A short marriage has been defined as one of less than 5 years, during which time no child has been born to the couple.

 

Small Business Corporation -- SBC

Small business corporations are exempt from the income attribution rules that apply to loan and transfers made to corporations with a spouse or minor children as shareholders. Sale of shares qualifies for the $500,000 lifetime capital gains exemption.

 

Small Business Deduction -- SBD)

 

Small Business Tax Rate

When an incorporated Canadian company has net active income below $200,000 it is eligible for a low tax rate.

 

Specific Performance

A type of remedy to a breach of contract whereby the courts order delivery of specific assets rather than compensation in cash. Not acceptable for personal contracts or contracts that require ongoing supervision.

 

Specific Perils

A type of automobile insurance that provides coverage against specific events or occurrences.

 

Specified Investment Business -- SIB

An incorporated business whose principal purpose is to derive passive income, income from investments, rental income, and royalties. Unless the corporation has more than 5 full time employees the corporation cannot claim a small business deduction.

 

Split annuity program

Under a split annuity program, the funds available for annuity purchase are split between an immediate term-certain annuity and a single-premium deferred annuity. The term-certain annuity provides a guaranteed income over a predetermined period. When the income from the term certain ceases, the full amount of the deferred annuity (principal and accumulated interest)

 

Spousal RRSP

A type of RRSP where one spouse contributes to a plan registered in the name of the other spouse. The contributed funds or assets belong to the beneficiary but the contributor gets the tax deduction. If the beneficiary removes funds from the spousal in the year of the contribution or in the subsequent 2 calendar years, the contributor pays the taxes.

 

Spousal Trust

A trust for a spouse. Certain conditions exist -- the spouse receives all the income; no assets can be distributed during the spouse's lifetime; and no taxes are triggered.

 

Spouse's Allowance

An indexed supplement to OAS for a widower, widow or spouse who is between the ages of 60 and 64 and meets an income test. Spouse's allowance is not taxable.

 

Standard Deviation

A statistical measure of risk. A measure of the variance against a mean. The higher the standard deviation the greater the risk.

 

Status Indians

A member of a first nation community whose name is entered on a band list. Contracts against these individuals are not enforceable.

 

Statute Barred

A provincial statute of limitations (6 years since the breach), which defines a maximum period after which the party suffering the breach cannot enforce the obligation.

 

Statutes

Federal or provincial acts, that prescribe legal conduct, define crimes, and in general sets rules of law in specified areas.

 

Stock Power of Attorney

A type of Power of Attorney that deals with the transferring of ownership in a share or shares.

 

Stop Loss Rules

Revenue Canada rules that have been established to prevent the creation of losses by the sale of assets for less than their fair market value.

 

Straight Life Annuity

A type of annuity that continues to be paid to the beneficiary as long as the beneficiary lives. The good news is that you cannot outlive the benefits. Payments are made in equal monthly, quarterly, semi-annual or annual amounts. Currently life annuities are only available through insurance companies.

 

Strata Title

In this case a condominium owner is a tenant in common of the common areas, but owns his/her own unit in fee simple.

 

Strong-Form Efficient

A strong-form efficient market holds that security prices reflect all public and private information.

 

Subrogation

An insurance clause that gives the insurance company the right to recover losses from a third party. For example -- a tenant causes a fire in his apartment. The owner recovers damages from the insurance company and the insurance company attempts to recover the damages from the tenant.

 

Successoral Planning

The Quebec equivalent to estate planning. It deals with the transmission of property at death.

 

Suicide Clause

A clause in an insurance contract whereby if death by suicide occurs within 2 years of the policy date, the insurer is not required to pay the death benefit.

 

Survivorship Condition

A clause in a will that specifies that the spouse must survive a minimum period of time (typically 30 days) after the testator's death. It is inserted to avoid possible double probate costs. a.k.a. Common Disaster Clause or Extreme Contingency Clause

 

Systematic Risk

Risk that cannot be diversified away because it cannot be controlled by an investor or fund manager. Often called nondiversifiable risk, or market risk (equities), or interest rate risk fixed income securities).

T

 

Take it or leave it

A CMHC mortgage can be taken by the client to a new location.

 

Tax Election Clause

A clause in a will that allows the executor to make the most advantageous tax election on behalf of an estate.

 

Tenancy in Common

Land ownership by two or more persons, but upon death the interest of the deceased tenant's ownership transfers to heirs. Only Unity of Possession (each party is entitled to undivided possession) applies.

 

Tenant in Fee Simple

Land that is held absolutely and forever by a person or his or her heirs.

 

Tenant's Package

A type of homeowners insurance that protects a tenant. It does not include building coverage and the applicant specifies the amount of personal property required.

 

Term Certain Annuities

A type of annuity that pays a beneficiary or in the event of their death, their estate a fixed amount for a fixed period of time. Guaranteed periods range from five to twenty years or until the annuitant(s) turns 90.

 

Term Insurance

A type of insurance that covers an individual for a specific time frame. It is characterized by low initial premiums that rise over time. There is no savings component and is considered as pure insurance.

 

Term Insurance Rider

A life insurance rider that allows the policy owner to purchase additional term insurance at a lower cost.

 

Terminal Loss

If you sell a depreciable business asset (buildings, trucks etc,) for less than the Undepreciated capital cost, Revenue Canada believes that you did not claim enough depreciation in prior years, and you are now entitled to a one time terminal loss or business loss

 

Testamentary Expenses

Expenses associated with a will.

 

Testamentary Trust

The transfer of property of a spousal trust by way of a will. Taxes are deferred until the death of the recipient.

 

Testator

The writer of a will.

 

Third Party Liability

A type of automobile insurance coverage that pays for bodily injury and property damages to third parties. Most provinces stipulate a minimum required amount.

 

Tort Law

A body of law, which establishes wrong and which will award an injured party damages for the injury. A tort is a civil wrong other than a breach of contract.

 

Treynor Index

A measure of performance developed for the evaluation of mutual fund performance. It was reasoned that in a CAPM world all diversifiable risk could be eliminated, leaving beta as the appropriate measure of risk. Therefore the relevant measure would be the greatest return per unit of risk taken. The higher the value the better. The index was calculated as -- PIT,I = Ri -r

 

Trust

An arrangement whereby assets are legally held by one party for the use of another, known as the beneficiary. The person who created the trust is known as the settlor and the administrator is the trustee.

 

Trustee

The person who holds legal title to the assets of a trust.

 

Tutor

A Quebec succession term that describes the person who may act as the guardian for a deceased person's minor child.

 

21-year rule

At the end of 21 years a trust must pay taxes on all accumulated capital gains. Revenue Canada deems the assets were disposed of, and gains may be taxed.

U

 

Undepreciated Capital Cost -- UCC

 

Underwriting

The purchase for resale of a security issue by one or more investment dealers or underwriters. The formal agreements pertaining to such a transaction are called underwriting agreements.

 

Undue Influence

Influence of another that prevents or interferes with another's free will. One party holds a dominant position over another. The burden of proof of undue influence is upon the party alleging it. Common sense practice is to use independent legal advice by each party.

 

Unilateral Contract

A type of contract where one of the parties can walk away from the contract without penalty. In the case of insurance contracts the insured can discontinue the policy (contract) at any time but the insurance company cannot if the client lives up to the contract.

 

Unities of Possession, Time, Title, and Interest

Ownership rights associated with joint tenancy arrangements.

 

Unity of Interest

Each party has the same rights (interest).

 

Unity of Possession

Each party is entitled to undivided possession of the entire estate.

 

Unity of Time

All joint tenants must be in possession of the land concurrently.

 

Unity of Title

Each party obtains title under the same instrument or deed.

 

Universal Life -- UL

A type of insurance policy with two parts-an insurance part and a savings component or an investment account. The interest earned on the account is similar to that earned on a savings account. Premiums of this type of policy can be increases or decreased.

 

Unsystematic Risk

Often called non-market or specific risk is the risk associated with a specific company.

 

Unused RRSP Deduction Room

The amount of RRSP that can be carried forward from previous years. For example if a client was entitled to place $13,500 in an RRSP and only contributed $10,000, the difference of $3,500 would be this.

 

Usufruct

A Quebec term that refers to the fact that an individual other than the owner, may have use of a property.

 

Usufructuary Rights

Interests that provide for the use of property that belongs to another. For example, the owner of land that borders on a waterway has the right to use the water. The public owns the water itself but the landholder has the right to use the water.

 

Usury

Charging exorbitant interest on a loan.

V

 

Variable Annuity

A type of deferred annuity whereby the payout varies with the returns earned on a seg fund.

 

Variable Life

A type of insurance contract that has both a death benefit and a cash surrender value. The value of cash surrender value is dependent upon the performance of the seg funds performance.

 

Variable Universal Life

A type of insurance policy similar to universal life but without a guaranteed minimum interest rate as the policyholder can choose between investments in stocks, bonds, and money market instruments.

 

Vesting

The employees right to employer contributions made on his or her behalf during the employee's period of enrollment.

 

Vicarious Liability

A liability incurred by an individual from something done by a subordinate. If a financial planner assigns duties to a subordinate he or she does not escape the associated liability even though he or she may be innocent of any undertakings.

 

Void Contract

A type of contract that cannot be enforced since it is deemed to have never have existed. A contract without legal effect.

 

Voidable

A type of contract that can be rescinded or repudiated (set aside) by one party. A contract with a minor can be voided by the minor and not enforced.

W

 

Waivers of Premiums

A life insurance rider whereby the insurance company waives the policy premium should the client become disabled.

 

Weak-Form Efficiency

A market is weak-formed efficient if security prices reflect all historical information.

 

Will

A legal document setting forth the wishes of the writer for the disposition of an estate after death.

 

Workers Compensation Board -- WCB

A provincial board that provides payments for permanent injuries sustained in the workplace.

Y

 

Year's Basic Exemption -- YBE

Contributing to CPP/QPP is compulsory for almost all Canadians. All self-employed persons making over $3,500 (1997)-the YBE must also pay in.

 

Year's Maximum Pensionable Earnings -- YMPE

The maximum ceiling on earnings for CPP/QPP contributions. In 1997 it was $35,800.
